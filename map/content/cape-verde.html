Cape Verde is where you go when you're done messing around. These volcanic islands sit 350 miles off the west coast of Africa in the eastern Atlantic, directly in the path of the largest blue marlin migration on the planet. Over 25 fish exceeding 1,000 lbs have been landed here, and the ones that got away would rewrite the record book. This is not a tourist fishery. It's remote, the logistics are real, and the fishing is absolutely world-class.

Why Cape Verde

The Cape Verde archipelago straddles the Atlantic's main thermocline where warm equatorial water meets the cool Canary Current. That collision creates a nutrient-rich upwelling zone that holds enormous schools of skipjack tuna, flying fish, and squid. Blue marlin follow that bait, and they show up here in staggering numbers and size. Fish in the 500-800 lb class are routine during peak season. Granders are a realistic possibility on any given day.

The islands themselves are volcanic, with steep underwater drop-offs and seamounts that concentrate bait and predators. Sao Vicente and Santo Antao create a channel effect that funnels current and pelagics between the islands. The deep water starts close to shore, so you're fishing productive structure within 30 minutes of leaving the harbor.

How to Fish It

Blue Marlin Trolling

This is a lure fishery. High-speed trolling with big, heavy skirted lures in the 12-16 inch range is the primary method. Most boats run 6-lure spreads at 7-9 knots, working the edges of underwater ridges and temperature breaks between the islands. The marlin here are aggressive and tend to eat with full commitment. Hookup-to-bite ratios are among the best anywhere because these fish are feeding hard, not just window-shopping.

Standard setup is 130 lb stand-up or chair tackle with two-speed lever drag reels. Leaders run 300-400 lb mono or fluorocarbon. The captains know the structure intimately and will position the boat on specific ledges and drop-offs where marlin ambush bait schools moving through the channels. The key areas are the deep edges between Sao Vicente and Santo Antao, the seamounts north of Sal, and the shelf break running along the western islands.

Lure color selection matters here. The water is deep Atlantic blue with excellent clarity. Dark-over-light patterns (black/purple, blue/white) and natural baitfish imitations perform consistently. Heavier head weights help lures track true in the open ocean swells, which can build to 6-8 feet even on calm days.

Live Bait

Some crews pitch live skipjack or small yellowfin to marlin spotted on the surface or raised on teasers. This is a deadly technique here because the bait population is so dense that marlin are conditioned to eat tuna. A slow-trolled 3-5 lb skipjack on a circle hook with 130 lb tackle is about as close to a guaranteed eat as billfish fishing gets.

The tease-and-switch method also works well. Running hookless teasers to raise a marlin, then pulling the teaser while pitching a live bait or rigged ballyhoo to the fired-up fish. It takes a skilled crew to execute cleanly, but the captains who specialize in this approach put their anglers on fish consistently.

Bottom and Reef Fishing

When you need a break from trolling, the volcanic reef structure holds wahoo, almaco jack, African pompano, and various grouper species. Jigging the seamounts with metal jigs in the 200-400g range produces wahoo and amberjack on lighter tackle. Some boats carry popping gear for giant trevally that patrol the rocky shorelines.

When to Go

  • May-June: Season opener. Water temps climbing. First big marlin start showing. Less boat traffic.
  • July-August: Peak season. Highest concentration of blue marlin. Water temps 78-82°F. This is when the granders appear.
  • September: Late season but still productive. Occasional yellowfin tuna blitzes on the seamounts.
  • October-April: Off-season for marlin. Some wahoo and tuna available but most charter operations shut down.

Where to Stay

Mindelo on Sao Vicente is the main fishing port and the only real base for charter operations. It's a small harbor town with a handful of hotels, restaurants, and a functioning marina. Don't expect luxury resort infrastructure. Accommodations range from basic guesthouses to a few mid-range hotels in town. The marina at Mindelo can handle sport fishing boats and has fuel and basic supplies.

Getting there requires a connection through Lisbon or Dakar, then a local flight to Sao Vicente's Cesaria Evora Airport. Several US-based charter companies organize full-package trips including flights, accommodations, and boat days. If this is your first trip, booking through an established operator who handles the logistics is the smart play. The language is Portuguese, the currency is Cape Verdean escudo, and English is not widely spoken outside the fishing fleet.

Plan for at least 5-7 fishing days to justify the travel investment. Weather days happen (trade winds can blow 20+ knots), and you want enough shots at the water to connect with the fish that make this trip worthwhile. Most organized trips run 7-10 days of fishing, which gives a solid margin for weather and also lets you settle into the rhythm of the fishery.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 130 lb stand-up tackle, two-speed lever drag reels loaded with 130 lb mono or hollow-core braid (most provided by charter)
  • Lures: Large skirted trolling lures, 12-16 inch heads, dark colors (black/purple, blue/white) for the deep blue water
  • Live bait rigs: 12/0-14/0 circle hooks, 300-400 lb fluorocarbon leaders, 130 lb class rods
  • Wahoo/jigging: 50-80 lb conventional or spinning, metal jigs 200-400g, high-speed wahoo lures
  • Popping: Heavy spinning rod rated PE8-10, 200 lb braid, large stickbaits and poppers for GT
  • Essentials: Sun protection rated for equatorial exposure, seasickness meds (open Atlantic swells are no joke), and extra terminal tackle since resupply options are limited
