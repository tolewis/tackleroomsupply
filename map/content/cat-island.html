Cat Island is what the Bahamas looked like before the resorts moved in. Fifty miles of undeveloped coastline, a single main road, and more fish than people. The deep water off the south end holds billfish and wahoo, the flats on the western shore have bonefish that rarely see a fly, and you can wade fish for an entire day without seeing another angler. If you want remote Bahamas fishing without chartering a plane to the far southern islands, Cat Island delivers.

Why Cat Island

Cat Island sits on the eastern edge of the Great Bahama Bank with the Exuma Sound dropping to 6,000 feet just off its southern tip. That deep water access is the key. Billfish, wahoo, and dolphin run the edge from fall through spring, and the proximity of blue water to shore means short runs to the fishing grounds. Hawk's Nest Creek on the south end has the only marina, which keeps the pressure low.

The western shore faces the shallow bank and offers miles of wadeable bonefish flats. These are not guide-circuit flats. The fish here see very few anglers, which means they behave differently. They feed confidently, they don't spook at 80 feet, and they eat a properly presented fly without the cat-and-mouse game you get on heavily fished flats. Average bonefish run 3-5 lbs, but fish over 8 lbs show up with regularity.

Cat Island also has the highest point in the Bahamas, Mount Alvernia at 206 feet, which tells you something about the island's character. It's quiet, it's real, and the fishing reflects that.

How to Fish It

Offshore

The deep water south of Hawk's Nest is the primary offshore zone. Trolling along the 100-fathom edge with a spread of rigged ballyhoo and skirted lures produces sailfish, white marlin, and the occasional blue marlin. Wahoo run the same ledges, particularly during the winter months when water temps drop into the mid-70s. High-speed trolling with Yo-Zuri Bonita lures or rigged horse ballyhoo at 14-16 knots is the standard wahoo approach.

Dolphin show up on the weed lines and current rips from April through August. Yellowfin tuna are present on the deeper structure, especially around the seamounts southeast of the island. Vertical jigging with butterfly jigs in 200-400 feet pulls them up when they won't hit topwater.

Flats

The western flats run for miles along the leeward shore, accessible by skiff from Hawk's Nest or by wading from several beach access points. The bottom is hard sand and turtle grass, firm enough to wade comfortably in flats boots. Fish incoming tide when bonefish push onto the shallow feeding grounds.

Fly anglers use 7-8 wt rods with #4-6 Gotcha, Crazy Charlie, or bead-chain Charlies in tan or pink. Spin anglers throw 1/8 oz jigs tipped with fresh shrimp or DOA shrimp on 8-10 lb line. The fish here are not leader-shy, so 12 lb fluorocarbon tippet is fine.

Permit are present on the deeper flats and around coral heads, particularly on the south end. They're not as reliable as Andros permit, but if you see them, a live crab on a light jighead or a Merkin crab fly on a 9-10 wt rod gives you a legitimate shot.

Shore & Reef

The rocky shoreline on the east (windward) side holds barracuda, jacks, and snapper. Casting plugs and spoons from the shore during the first two hours of incoming tide can produce aggressive strikes. Reef fishing in 30-60 feet off the south end produces yellowtail snapper and mutton snapper on cut bait.

When to Go

  • November-February: Peak wahoo. Sailfish on the deep edges. Bonefish concentrated on the flats in cooler water. Best weather for offshore runs.
  • March-April: Billfish season builds. Permit showing on the deeper flats. Calm conditions for wading.
  • May-July: Dolphin and yellowfin offshore. Flats fishing stays productive. Water warms but wind drops.
  • August-October: Hurricane season limits offshore days. Shore fishing and flats remain fishable between systems. Fewer visitors, lower rates.

Where to Stay

Hawk's Nest Resort and Marina on the south end is the base for serious anglers. It has the only marina on the island, fuel, a small fleet of charter boats, and basic but comfortable rooms. The restaurant serves Bahamian food and cold beer, and the staff can arrange flats guides. Fernandez Bay Village on the west shore is a beachfront option with cottages, though you'll need to arrange fishing separately. For the truly self-sufficient, rental houses in New Bight and Arthur's Town offer kitchens and proximity to wadeable flats, but you'll need your own gear and local knowledge.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Billfish trolling: 30-50 lb stand-up combos, rigged ballyhoo, skirted lures, 7/0-9/0 circle hooks
  • Wahoo: 50-80 lb class, high-speed lures, single-strand wire or cable leaders
  • Bonefishing (fly): 7-8 wt rod, floating line, #4-6 Gotcha/Crazy Charlie/bead-chain Charlie
  • Bonefishing (spin): Light spinning, 8-10 lb braid, 12 lb fluoro leader, 1/8 oz jigs with shrimp
  • Permit: 9-10 wt fly rod or medium spinning, live crabs, Merkin crab patterns
  • Shore casting: Medium spinning, 15-20 lb line, topwater plugs, silver spoons, wire leader for barracuda
