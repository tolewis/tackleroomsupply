Tahiti is where big-game fishing started. In 1930, Zane Grey came here and caught a 1,040 lb striped marlin off Tahiti's coast, putting the territory on the world fishing map for good. Nearly a century later, French Polynesia remains one of the most productive blue-water fisheries on the planet. The deep volcanic drop-offs, remote coral atolls, and warm equatorial currents create a concentration of pelagic species that few places can match. This is not a casual fishing trip. This is the deep end.

Why Tahiti

French Polynesia spans an ocean area the size of Western Europe, spread across 118 islands in five archipelagos. The fishing divides into three distinct zones, each world-class in its own right.

The waters around Tahiti and Moorea drop to 3,000 feet within a mile of shore. Blue marlin cruise these volcanic drop-offs year-round, with fish in the 400-800 lb class common and granders taken every season. Yellowfin tuna, bigeye tuna, wahoo, and mahimahi fill out the offshore calendar.

The Tuamotu Archipelago, a chain of 78 low-lying coral atolls northeast of Tahiti, is something else entirely. The passes between lagoon and open ocean funnel baitfish on every tide change, and the predators stack up. Giant trevally over 100 lbs, dogtooth tuna to 200 lbs, and yellowfin tuna prowl these passes. Popping and jigging in the Tuamotus is some of the most aggressive fishing on earth.

The Austral Islands, 400 miles south of Tahiti, offer bonefishing on remote atolls that see almost zero fishing pressure. These are not Bahamas-style guided flats programs. This is expedition fishing on untouched sand flats where bonefish have never seen a fly.

How to Fish It

Offshore - Tahiti & Moorea

Blue marlin fishing around the main islands is primarily trolling with lure spreads along the volcanic shelves. Captains run 6-rod spreads with large skirted lures in the 10-14 inch head range, targeting marlin in the 400-1,000 lb class. Tackle is 80-130 lb class, stand-up or chair, depending on the boat and the angler. The bite windows are early morning and late afternoon, with fish often surfacing on the temperature breaks a mile or two offshore.

Yellowfin tuna are abundant and big. Fish in the 100-150 lb range show up regularly on trolled lures, chunk baits, and poppers. Bigeye tuna hold deeper, in the 300-600 foot range, and respond to deep-drop rigs or slow-pitch jigs worked on the bottom. Wahoo run the reef edges and are caught both trolling and casting.

Atoll Passes - Tuamotus

The Tuamotu atolls are the reason serious anglers fly halfway around the world. The passes connecting the lagoons to the open Pacific create a conveyor belt of bait and predators on every tide change. Outgoing tide is the prime window, when baitfish flush from the lagoon and every predator in the area lines up to feed.

Giant trevally are the marquee target. Popping with large stickbaits and surface plugs on heavy spinning tackle (PE6-8 rods, 100 lb braid, 150 lb fluorocarbon leaders) pulls GTs in the 50-120 lb range out of the pass mouths. Dogtooth tuna patrol the deeper sides of the passes and respond to high-speed jigs and trolled Rapala Magnums. Yellowfin tuna feed in the same zones, often competing with the GTs on the surface.

Mothership charters are the standard way to fish the Tuamotus. Vessels like the Ultimate Lady run 7-10 day itineraries covering multiple atolls, anchoring off the passes and running tenders into the fishing zones. This is not a day-trip fishery. The logistics require a live-aboard approach.

Flats - Austral Islands

The Austral Islands hold bonefish on shallow coral and sand flats that are virtually unfished. Access is by inter-island flight from Tahiti followed by boat transfers. Fly anglers use 8-9 wt rods (step up from Bahamas weight, since the fish are bigger and the coral is unforgiving). Patterns that work: Crazy Charlie, Christmas Island Specials, and crab patterns in #4-6. Leaders need to be heavier than Caribbean standard, 16-20 lb tippet, to handle the coral.

Jigging & Popping - General

Slow-pitch jigging and speed jigging produce throughout French Polynesia. Around seamounts and deep structure, 200-400 gram jigs worked on heavy spinning or conventional tackle pull up dogtooth tuna, amberjack, ruby snapper, and jobfish from 200-600 feet. This is physically demanding fishing that rewards proper technique and good conditioning.

When to Go

  • November-April: Prime blue marlin season around Tahiti. Warm water, active pelagics, best GT fishing in the Tuamotus. This is the wet season, so expect afternoon squalls, but mornings are typically clear.
  • May-July: Transition period. Marlin fishing remains productive. Yellowfin tuna peak. Cooler, drier weather makes for comfortable days. Good window for mothership charters in the Tuamotus.
  • August-October: Cooler water period. Offshore slows slightly for billfish but wahoo picks up. Jigging for dogtooth tuna remains strong year-round. Austral bonefish flats are fishable year-round, with May through October offering calmer wind.

Where to Stay

For Tahiti-based offshore fishing, Taina Marina in Punaauia is the charter hub with fuel, slips, and direct access to the marlin grounds. The InterContinental Tahiti and Manava Suite Resort are both close to the marina. For Moorea, the Hilton Moorea has a small marina and access to local charter boats.

For the Tuamotus, mothership charters are the practical option. The Ultimate Lady and similar vessels run scheduled itineraries from Tahiti, covering Rangiroa, Fakarava, Makemo, and other atolls. Rangiroa also has small lodges (Kia Ora Resort, Rangiroa Lodge) with local guides who fish the passes by skiff.

The Austral Islands are expedition territory. Rurutu and Tubuai have basic guesthouses, but most anglers access the flats via guided expedition packages that handle logistics end to end.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up or chair combos, large skirted lures (10-14 inch heads), 400 lb wind-on leaders, 10/0-12/0 circle hooks
  • Yellowfin/bigeye tuna: 50-80 lb spinning or conventional, poppers, stickbaits, butterfly jigs, chunking rigs with 7/0 circles
  • Giant trevally: PE6-8 popping rods, 100 lb braid, 150 lb fluorocarbon, large stickbaits and poppers (60-200 gram)
  • Dogtooth tuna: Heavy jigging rods, 80-100 lb braid, 200-400 gram speed jigs, assist hooks
  • Bonefishing (fly): 8-9 wt rod, floating tropical line, 16-20 lb tippet, #4-6 Crazy Charlie/Christmas Island Special/crab patterns
  • Slow-pitch jigging: Dedicated slow-pitch rod, 50-80 lb braid, 200-400 gram jigs, twin-assist hooks
  • General: Bring spare everything. Tackle shops do not exist in the Tuamotus or Australs. Replace all hooks and split rings before the trip.
