Kona's reputation is built on one thing: giant blue marlin. The leeward coast of Hawaii's Big Island drops to abyssal depths within a mile of shore, creating a deep-water fishery unlike anywhere else on Earth. No 60-mile canyon runs — you're in 1,000 fathoms before your coffee gets cold.

Why Kona

166 granders (marlin over 1,000 lbs) have been caught here. Nine World Cup victories. The Kona fishery produces more giant blue marlin per trip than any port in the Pacific. The combination of warm water, underwater volcanic structure, deep current upwelling, and a massive bait population (opelu, akule, flying fish) creates the perfect marlin habitat.

Beyond marlin, Kona delivers consistent yellowfin and bigeye tuna, ono (wahoo), mahi-mahi, and striped marlin. Short-billed spearfish are common here too — a species rarely seen elsewhere.

How to Fish It

Marlin

Kona-style trolling is a discipline unto itself. High-speed lure trolling at 8-10 knots with jet-head and plunger-style lures is the standard. The classic Kona head — a slant-face, flat-bottom design — was invented here. Boats run 6-lure spreads in a staggered pattern, fishing the surface wash and wave crests.

Heavy tackle: 80-130 lb stand-up or chair gear, depending on the charter. Most Kona boats are fully rigged with gold-anodized reels, gimbal belts, and fighting chairs. Captain and crew will handle the rigging — your job is to be ready when the reel screams.

Bottom & Light Tackle

When the marlin bite is slow, Kona boats often switch to bottom fishing on the ledges for ulua (giant trevally), various snapper species, and amberjack. Light-tackle jigging for ono and mahi is also popular on the run out.

When to Go

  • June–September: Peak blue marlin season. The big females move through in summer.
  • October: Hawaiian International Billfish Tournament (HIBT) — one of the oldest and most prestigious.
  • January–March: Striped marlin peak. Yellowfin tuna on the bite.
  • Year-round: Ono, mahi, and smaller blue marlin are caught every month.

Where to Stay

Honokohau Harbor is ground zero — most charter boats operate from here. The harbor has fuel, bait, a launch ramp, and the Bite Me Fish Market for fresh ahi. Hotels in Kailua-Kona town are a 5-minute drive. For full-service fishing lodges, Kona has several operators that package accommodations + charter days.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up or chair tackle (provided by charter)
  • Lures: Kona-style jet heads, plungers, bullet heads — 12-16" sizes for blues
  • Ono/mahi: 30-50 lb spinning or conventional, high-speed trolling lures
  • Bottom: 50-80 lb conventional with 8-16 oz leads, cut bait
