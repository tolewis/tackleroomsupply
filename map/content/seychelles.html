The Seychelles archipelago scatters across 500,000 square miles of the western Indian Ocean, surrounded by some of the cleanest, bluest water on Earth. The fishing here splits into two completely different worlds: bluewater billfish and tuna off the main islands, and flats fishing for giant trevally on remote outer atolls that look like they haven't changed in a thousand years.

Why Seychelles

Start with the giant trevally. The outer atolls of the Seychelles (Alphonse, Cosmoledo, Farquhar, Providence, Astove) are the pinnacle of GT fly fishing on the planet. Nowhere else can you consistently sight-cast to 80-120 lb giant trevally on white sand flats in crystal-clear water. The fish are aggressive, the setting is prehistoric, and the experience is unlike any other in the sport.

Then there's the bluewater. Blue marlin and black marlin both patrol the deep water around the Seychelles banks. Yellowfin tuna run big here, with fish over 100 lbs common. Dogtooth tuna, one of the hardest-fighting reef fish alive, lurk on the drop-offs and seamounts. Sailfish, wahoo, and giant trevally on the surface round out a species list that reads like a catalog of everything an angler could want.

This is not a budget trip. The Seychelles is remote, expensive, and requires planning. But the fishing is pristine in a way that barely exists anymore. Low pressure, healthy populations, and water clarity that has to be seen to be believed.

How to Fish It

Giant Trevally on Fly

The outer atoll fishery is wade-and-sight-cast on white sand and coral flats. You walk the flat with a guide, spot tailing or cruising GTs, and present a large fly (4-6 inch baitfish patterns, brush flies, or crab patterns) on a 12-weight fly rod with a fast-sinking line or intermediate tip.

GTs on the flats are explosive. The eat is violent. The first run will test your backing knot. You need a reel with a sealed drag that can handle sustained pressure and saltwater immersion. Plan on breaking fish off. These animals live in coral, and they will try to cut you off on every run.

Beyond GTs, the flats hold bonefish, bluefin trevally, triggerfish (both yellowmargin and moustache), milkfish, and Indo-Pacific permit. A week on Cosmoledo or Alphonse is a multi-species fly fishing marathon.

Bluewater Billfish and Tuna

Blue and black marlin fishing operates primarily out of Mahe and Praslin, with boats running to the offshore banks and seamounts. Trolling with lures and rigged skipjack, working the edges where the shelf drops into deep water. The marlin here tend to be in the 200-600 lb range, with the possibility of much larger fish, particularly black marlin from October through April.

Yellowfin tuna school on the banks and around FADs (fish aggregation devices) that the local fleet maintains. Popping and jigging for yellowfin in the 40-150 lb range is exceptional. Bring heavy popping rods. The tuna here are not pushovers, and GTs regularly crash the party.

Dogtooth Tuna

Dogtooth tuna are the sleeper species of the Seychelles. These reef-associated tuna hold on the steep drop-offs around the outer islands and seamounts, in 100-400 feet of water. Vertical jigging with 300-500g metal jigs on heavy conventional or PE6-8 spinning gear is the primary technique. Fish run 40-120 lbs and fight like a tuna crossed with an amberjack. They'll head straight for the reef on the hookset, so heavy drag and fast cranking are non-negotiable.

When to Go

  • March–April: Best weather window. Southeast trades haven't filled in yet. Peak GT fishing on the outer atolls. Calm seas for bluewater trips.
  • October–November: Second prime window. Inter-monsoon period with light winds and warm water. Black marlin moving through.
  • May–September: Southeast monsoon. Windier and rougher, especially on the outer atolls. Bluewater fishing still productive but less comfortable. Lower rates.
  • December–February: Northwest monsoon. Rain and variable winds. Some atoll operations close. Marlin fishing continues off Mahe.
  • Year-round: Yellowfin tuna, dogtooth tuna, GTs on the reef edges, and wahoo. The fishery never shuts down, it just shifts emphasis.

Where to Stay

Mahe is the main island and international gateway. Seychelles International Airport (SEZ) receives direct flights from Dubai, Doha, Addis Ababa, and several European cities. Mahe has the largest charter fleet and most accessible bluewater fishing. Victoria, the capital, has marina facilities and a handful of sport fishing operators.

For the outer atolls, access is by small charter plane or live-aboard mothership. Alphonse Island has a dedicated fishing lodge (Alphonse Island Lodge) that runs guided GT and bluewater trips. Cosmoledo and Farquhar operate as fly-in fishing camps with limited capacity, typically 6-12 anglers per week. These book 12-18 months in advance.

Budget accordingly. A week on an outer atoll runs $8,000-15,000 per person including flights, guiding, meals, and accommodation. Bluewater charters out of Mahe are more accessible at $1,500-2,500/day. The Seychelles is not a place you visit on impulse. Plan ahead, book early, and treat it as the trip of a lifetime.

Tackle Recommendations

  • GT fly fishing: 12-weight fly rod (9 ft), large arbor reel with 25+ lb drag and sealed internals, 400-grain sinking line or tropical intermediate, 80-100 lb fluorocarbon bite tippet, large baitfish/brush flies (4/0-6/0)
  • GT popping/spinning: Heavy popping rod (PE6-8), 14000-18000 size spinning reel, 80-100 lb braid, 130-150 lb fluorocarbon leader, large poppers and stick baits
  • Blue/black marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up or chair tackle (provided by charter), skirted lures, rigged skipjack
  • Yellowfin tuna: 50-80 lb spinning or conventional, poppers, stick baits, 200-400g jigs
  • Dogtooth tuna: PE6-8 jigging rod, heavy conventional or spinning reel, 300-500g metal jigs, 150 lb fluorocarbon leader
  • Bonefish/flats species: 8-9 weight fly rod, tropical floating line, standard bonefish and permit patterns
