The New Jersey coast from Raritan Bay to Cape May produces some of the biggest striped bass on the East Coast every spring, and kayak anglers are right in the middle of it. While the party boats crowd the channels and the surf guys fight for beach access, kayak fishermen paddle into skinny water, rocky structure, and tidal rips that hold 40-plus pound bass within a mile of shore. It's the most accessible big-striper fishery you can reach from a car-top kayak, and the catches rival anything coming off boats five times the size.

Why New Jersey Kayak Fishing

New Jersey sits on the spring migration highway. Striped bass push north from the Chesapeake Bay and Virginia coast starting in April, following bunker schools and warming water up the Jersey shore. The fish stack up in predictable locations: rocky points, jetties, inlet mouths, and sandy troughs where bait concentrates on moving tides. From a kayak, you can access structure that's unfishable from shore and too shallow for charter boats. You're putting baits and lures directly on top of fish that rarely see pressure.

The spring run delivers stripers in the 20 to 50 pound class, with legitimate shots at fish over 40 pounds from April through June. That's a trophy-class bass from a 12-foot kayak in open water. It demands skill, preparation, and respect for the conditions, but the payoff is extraordinary.

How to Fish It

Bait Fishing from the Kayak

Clam bellies, bunker chunks, and live eels are the three baits that catch the most big stripers from kayaks in New Jersey. Clam bellies fished on a fishfinder rig (3/0 to 5/0 circle hook, 40-pound fluorocarbon leader, 2 to 4 oz bank sinker on a slider) are deadly in the spring. Anchor up-current of a sandy trough or drop-off and let the clam wash into the strike zone. Bunker chunks work the same rig but put out more scent in dirty water. Live eels on a simple 5/0 circle hook with no weight are the nighttime and low-light weapon. Drift or slow-paddle eels along rocky structure and jetty edges on an outgoing tide.

Casting Artificials

When stripers are actively feeding on bunker schools, casting artificials from a kayak is explosive. Large swimbaits (6 to 9 inches) in bunker patterns cover the most water. The Hogy Original in olive/white and the BKD Herring are local favorites. Metal lip swimmers like the Daiwa SP Minnow in 6-inch size work well on a slow retrieve over structure. For topwater, a large pencil popper or walk-the-dog plug during a bunker blitz will get smashed. Fish 40 to 50-pound braid on a 3000 to 4000-size spinning reel with a 30 to 40-pound fluorocarbon leader.

Key Spots

Raritan Bay and Sandy Hook

Union Beach launches directly onto Raritan Bay flats that hold stripers from mid-April through June. Paddle south toward the Earle Naval Pier area where the bottom drops from 6 to 15 feet along a sand edge. Clam bellies on a fishfinder rig anchored on the drop produce big fish on the incoming tide. The Bayshore Waterfront Park launch is the go-to access point.

Cliffwood Beach offers access to the same Raritan Bay structure from the western side. The rocky bottom near the old pier foundation holds bass on both tides. This spot fishes well on an outgoing tide when bait flushes out of the Matawan Creek channel.

Southern Jersey

Corson's Inlet between Ocean City and Strathmere is a kayak-friendly launch with access to back-bay flats and the inlet mouth. Stripers and weakfish hold on the oyster beds and channel edges in the back bay, while bigger bass patrol the inlet on moving tides. The state park launch provides easy access.

Cape May marks the southern end of the spring migration and offers excellent kayak fishing around the Cape May Reef, the concrete ship (SS Atlantus), and the rip off Cape May Point. This area gets the first fish in spring and the last fish in fall. Big stripers hold on the reef structure in 15 to 30 feet of water, reachable by kayak on calm days.

When to Go

  • April: The run begins. First fish show in Raritan Bay and around Sandy Hook by mid-April. Schoolies and slot fish arrive first, with bigger cows following by late April. Water temps hitting 50 to 52 degrees triggers the push. Clam bellies are the top bait this early.
  • May: Peak month. Trophy-class fish in the 30 to 50 pound range are realistic from Raritan Bay through Long Beach Island. Bunker schools arrive and the bass follow them. Morning and evening bites are strongest. This is the month to take off work.
  • June: Big fish start pushing north but holdovers feed in deeper structure through mid-June. Topwater blitzes on bunker schools are possible in early June. By late June, the spring run is winding down and the focus shifts to schoolie bass and fluke.
  • September through November: Fall run. Fish push south again. Not the same trophy size as spring but consistent action on schoolies and slot fish. October is the strongest month for the fall migration. Live eels on jetty edges are deadly.

Where to Stay

Union Beach and Keyport in Raritan Bay have budget motels and Airbnb options within minutes of the best launch points. Further south, Ocean City is a dry town with family-friendly rentals and easy access to Corson's Inlet. Cape May has bed-and-breakfasts, hotels, and vacation rentals, plus some of the best restaurants on the Jersey coast.

For kayak launches, you need easy water access and parking that can handle a roof-topped boat. Bayshore Waterfront Park (Union Beach), Cliffwood Beach, Corson's Inlet State Park, and Cape May's Poverty Beach area all work.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Primary rod and reel: 7-foot medium-heavy spinning rod. 4000-size reel (Shimano Stradic, Penn Battle III, or Daiwa BG). 40-pound braid, 30 to 40-pound fluorocarbon leader. This covers bait fishing, casting, and light trolling.
  • Bait rigs: Fishfinder rig with 36-inch 40-pound fluorocarbon leader, 3/0 to 5/0 Gamakatsu Octopus circle hooks, 2 to 4 oz bank sinkers on a fish-finder slider. Pre-tie a dozen before each trip.
  • Clam bellies: Buy fresh surf clams from a bait shop the morning of your trip. Salted clam bellies work as backup. Thread the entire belly onto the hook so the barb sits in the tough foot section.
  • Bunker chunks: Fresh bunker (menhaden) cut into 2 to 3 inch chunks. Hook through the backbone section for best hold on the hook.
  • Eels: Live eels on a 5/0 circle hook, no weight. Fish them on a slow drift or paddle. Store in a small mesh bag clipped to the kayak to keep them alive and accessible.
  • Lures: Hogy Original 7-inch (olive/white), Daiwa SP Minnow (bunker color), white bucktail jigs in 1 to 2 oz, large pencil poppers for topwater blitzes.
  • Safety gear: PFD worn at all times (required by NJ law for kayaks). Whistle, paddle leash, anchor system with quick-release, VHF radio or waterproof phone case, and a flag/light visible to powerboats. Do not skip the PFD. Cold water in April and May can kill you in minutes if you capsize without one.
