Guatemala's Pacific coast doesn't get the tourism traffic of Costa Rica or Mexico, and that's part of why the fishing is so absurd. The waters off Iztapa and Puerto Quetzal consistently produce the highest sailfish-per-day release numbers on the planet. We're talking 40-plus release days in peak season, with some boats logging 60 or 70 in a single outing. If you want to pull on sailfish until your arms quit, this is where you go.

Why Guatemala

Geography does most of the work. The continental shelf drops fast off Guatemala's Pacific coast, putting blue water within 20 to 30 miles of the marina. A deep submarine canyon funnels nutrient-rich upwelling right along the shelf edge, stacking baitfish in predictable lanes. Sailfish follow that bait in enormous concentrations from November through April. The sheer density of fish here is hard to overstate. Boats regularly raise 30 to 50 sails a day, and double-digit hookups before lunch are normal during peak weeks.

Beyond the sailfish circus, Guatemala produces legitimate blue marlin in the summer months. Fish in the 300 to 500 pound class show up on the shelf edge and around offshore structure from May through September. Yellowfin tuna, mahi-mahi, and wahoo fill in the gaps. But let's be honest: people come here for the sails, and the sails deliver.

How to Fish It

Sailfish on Teasers and Pitch Baits

The Guatemalan fleet has refined the teaser-and-pitch technique to an art form. Boats run a spread of hookless teasers: dredges, daisy chains, and squid spreader bars that create a commotion in the spread. When a sailfish lights up on a teaser, the mate pulls it away while the angler pitches a rigged ballyhoo or live bait into the fish's face. Circle hooks are standard, and the bite-to-release ratio is excellent. Most boats fish 20 to 30 pound conventional tackle, which keeps the fight sporting and the releases quick.

Fly Fishing for Sailfish

Guatemala is one of the best places in the world to catch a sailfish on fly. The teaser technique sets up perfectly for it. The mate teases the fish close to the transom, the fly angler strips a large billfish fly into the zone, and the sail eats. A 12-weight outfit with a fast-sinking shooting head and a large deceiver or popper pattern is the standard rig. Some boats specialize in fly fishing and carry anglers who release 10-plus sails on fly in a single day. That's not a typo.

Blue Marlin

Summer marlin fishing runs heavier tackle. Boats troll large lures and rigged bonito on 50 to 80-pound outfits along the shelf edge, working the same canyon structure that holds sailfish in winter. Blue marlin in the 300 to 600 pound range are realistic targets. The marlin fishery here doesn't get the press it deserves because the sailfish numbers dominate the conversation, but the summer bite is genuinely world-class.

Inshore Species

The inshore bite around the volcanic rock structure and river mouths holds roosterfish, jack crevalle, and snapper. It's secondary to the offshore action, but a half-day inshore trip makes a solid warm-up or cooldown day between offshore runs. Light spinning gear and live sardines cover most situations.

When to Go

  • November through January: Sailfish season builds fast. December is prime time, with 30 to 50 release days becoming routine. Seas are generally calm. Book well in advance for holiday weeks.
  • February through April: Peak sailfish continues. February and March often produce the highest single-day numbers. Water temperatures stay in the 80 to 84 degree range. Mahi-mahi show in better numbers as the season progresses.
  • May through September: Sailfish taper off but blue marlin pick up. June through August is prime marlin time. Yellowfin tuna in the 30 to 80 pound range show on current edges and FADs. Afternoon rain squalls are common but mornings fish clean.
  • October: Transition month. Some early sailfish start showing, marlin winding down. Lower charter rates and availability. Seas can be choppy.

Where to Stay

Iztapa is the fishing hub. It's a small, working-class Guatemalan town on the coast, not a resort destination. The fishing lodges here are purpose-built for anglers: Casa Vieja Lodge, Sailfish Bay Lodge, and Fins n Feathers are the big names. They run all-inclusive packages that cover lodging, meals, open bar, and charter boats. That's the way to do it. Trying to piece together independent travel and boat charters in Iztapa adds hassle with no real savings.

Puerto Quetzal is the commercial port nearby and serves as the departure point for some charter operations. It has less angler infrastructure than Iztapa but sits closer to the highway from Guatemala City.

Getting there: Fly into La Aurora International Airport in Guatemala City. Iztapa is about a two-hour drive south through the highlands and down to the Pacific lowlands. Most lodges arrange airport transfers. Guatemala City itself isn't a place to linger, so plan your arrival to connect straight to the coast.

One note on logistics: Guatemala is a developing country and the infrastructure outside Guatemala City reflects that. The fishing lodges are comfortable and well-run, but don't expect the polish of a Costa Rica resort. You're here for the fishing, and the fishing is unmatched.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Sailfish (conventional): 20 to 30-pound stand-up rods and reels. Penn International 30 or Shimano Tiagra 30 class. 30-pound mono or braid topshot. 30-pound fluorocarbon leaders, 7/0 circle hooks rigged on ballyhoo.
  • Sailfish (fly): 12-weight fly rod, 9 feet minimum. Large arbor saltwater reel with quality drag (Abel, Tibor, Hatch). 500-grain fast-sinking shooting head. Large billfish flies: Cam Sigler Rattler, Blanton's Flashtail Whistler in blue/white, 5/0 to 7/0 hooks.
  • Blue marlin: 50 to 80-pound stand-up outfits. Large trolling lures in the 10 to 14-inch range. 300-pound wind-on leaders. Heavy-duty harness and gimbal belt.
  • Terminal tackle: Mustad 39960 circle hooks (6/0 and 7/0 for sails, 10/0 for marlin). Spring-loaded dehooking tools for fast releases. Jinkai or Momoi fluorocarbon in 60 to 80 pound test.
  • Sun protection: Long-sleeve performance shirts, quality polarized sunglasses (Costa or Smith), and reef-safe sunscreen. The equatorial sun at sea level will cook you.
