Madeira has been quietly embarrassing every other blue marlin port in the Atlantic for decades. This Portuguese island 600 miles off the Moroccan coast has produced 58 granders and won the World Cup Blue Marlin Championship six times. That's more World Cup wins than any other port in history. The volcanic seamounts surrounding the island create a deepwater upwelling system that stacks bait and blue marlin within three miles of shore, and the European charter fleet that fishes here knows exactly how to exploit it.

Why Madeira

The underwater geography is the whole story. Madeira sits atop a massive volcanic seamount chain, and the island's steep submarine cliffs drop from the shoreline to over 6,000 feet within a few miles. When the North Atlantic current hits these underwater walls, it forces cold, nutrient-rich water to the surface. That upwelling feeds an enormous bait population of mackerel, skipjack, and squid right against the island. Blue marlin follow that bait in from the open Atlantic and stack up on the seamount edges.

The short run to productive water is a major advantage. You're fishing in under 30 minutes from Funchal harbor. No 60-mile run to the canyons, no burning half your day in transit. That means more lines in the water, more shots at fish. The island also produces excellent bigeye tuna, with fish regularly exceeding 200 lbs on the seamount drops.

How to Fish It

Blue Marlin Lure Trolling

The Madeira fleet runs high-speed trolling with large skirted lures, similar to the Azores and Kona approaches but adapted to local conditions. Standard spread is 5-6 lures trolled at 7-9 knots along the south coast drop-offs and over the submarine ridges. The captains work specific temperature breaks where 72-74°F surface water meets the cooler upwelling. Lure color preferences lean toward blue/white, green/yellow, and black/red combinations in the 10-14 inch range.

The tackle standard is 80-130 lb class, with most serious boats rigged with 130 lb stand-up gear and two-speed reels. The marlin here are big. Average blue is 400-600 lbs, with multiple fish over 800 lbs boated each season. When a grander shows up, you want the heavy tackle.

Live Baiting

Some of the most successful Madeira captains combine lure trolling with a live bait stinger on the rigger. Fresh-caught skipjack tuna in the 3-6 lb range, bridled on a circle hook, rides behind the lure spread. This bait-and-switch technique accounts for a disproportionate number of the island's biggest fish. The trick is keeping live bait in the well while covering ground with lures.

When a marlin is spotted or raised on teasers, the crew will pitch the live bait directly to the fish while pulling the teasers clear. The eat on a pitched live bait is usually immediate and violent. Circle hooks set themselves in the corner of the jaw, making for clean releases on fish you don't intend to weigh.

Bigeye Tuna

The seamounts hold serious bigeye tuna, particularly in the 150-300 lb range. Deep dropping with weighted rigs and chunk bait (squid, mackerel) at night on the seamount edges is the primary method. Some boats target bigeye on the drift with live bait at 200-400 foot depths during the day. This is outstanding big-game fishing in its own right, and a dedicated bigeye trip to Madeira would be worth the airfare even without the marlin.

When to Go

  • June: Season opens. Water temps rising through the mid-70s. First marlin showing on the seamounts. Bigeye tuna fishing strong.
  • July-August: Prime time. Highest blue marlin catch rates. Peak water temps 76-80°F. This is when the granders are most likely.
  • September: Late season, still very productive. Fewer boats on the water, which some anglers prefer. Bigeye tuna bite intensifies.
  • October: Season winding down. Some marlin still around but catch rates drop. Great for bigeye and wahoo.
  • November-May: Off-season for marlin. The island is beautiful year-round, but the big-game fleet largely stands down.

Where to Stay

Funchal is the main city and the base for all charter operations. The Funchal Marina is well-equipped with fuel, water, and docking for sport fishing boats. The city itself is a proper European destination with excellent hotels, restaurants, and infrastructure. This is one of the few world-class marlin ports where your non-fishing travel companions won't be bored stiff.

Madeira is famous for its wine (Madeira wine has been produced here since the 15th century), dramatic mountain scenery, levada hiking trails, and year-round mild climate. Direct flights from Lisbon take about 90 minutes, and several European carriers serve the island. Hotels in Funchal range from boutique properties in the old town to resort-style places on the waterfront. Book accommodations and charters well in advance for July and August.

The European charter fleet here is experienced and professional. Most captains speak English, Portuguese, and often German or French. Charter rates are generally lower than comparable US-based operations in the Caribbean or Hawaii, and the boats are well-maintained.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up tackle, two-speed lever drag reels (provided by most charters)
  • Lures: Skirted trolling lures 10-14 inches, blue/white and black/red preferred, medium to heavy jet heads
  • Live bait: 12/0-14/0 circle hooks, 300 lb fluorocarbon leaders, bridle rigging supplies
  • Bigeye tuna: 80 lb stand-up gear, 6/0-8/0 circle hooks, 150-200 lb fluorocarbon, glow sticks for night drops
  • Wahoo: High-speed trolling lures, 80 lb wire leaders, single-strand #15 wire or 200 lb fluorocarbon
  • Bring your own: Favorite wind-on leaders, crimps, and terminal tackle. Tackle shops in Funchal carry basics but selection is limited compared to US suppliers
