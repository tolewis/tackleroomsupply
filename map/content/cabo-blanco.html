Cabo Blanco sits at the northern tip of Peru where the cold Humboldt Current slams into warm equatorial water, and the result is one of the most nutrient-dense fishing grounds in the Pacific. This is where Alfred Glassell caught the all-tackle world record black marlin in 1953. That fish went 1,560 pounds. The record stood for decades, and Cabo Blanco still produces black marlin over 1,000 pounds. It's not easy to get here, the infrastructure is thin, and the fishing demands serious commitment. But if you want a shot at the biggest billfish in the ocean, this is hallowed ground.

Why Cabo Blanco

The Humboldt Current is the engine. It pushes cold, nutrient-loaded water north along the South American coast, and when that current meets warmer tropical water off northern Peru, the upwelling creates an explosion of marine life. Massive schools of bonito, skipjack, and anchoveta stack up in the convergence zone, and everything with teeth follows them. Black marlin, striped marlin, broadbill swordfish, yellowfin tuna, wahoo, and big dorado all feed in these waters.

The Cabo Blanco Fishing Club was the center of big-game fishing in the 1950s and 1960s, drawing anglers like Ernest Hemingway and hosting record catches that put Peru on the international fishing map. The club fell into disrepair after the 1972 El Nino disrupted fish patterns, and the area never regained its former fame. But the fish came back. The current still upwells, the bait still stacks, and the marlin still patrol the shelf edge. What Cabo Blanco lacks in amenities it makes up for in raw fishing potential that most anglers will never experience.

How to Fish It

Black Marlin

This is the main event. Black marlin here average 300 to 600 pounds, with fish over 1,000 pounds caught every season. The standard technique is trolling large rigged baits (bonito and skipjack in the 5 to 15 pound range) along the shelf edge and current breaks. Live bait fishing is devastatingly effective when you can locate a temperature break with bait stacked on it. Heavy tackle is mandatory: 80 to 130-pound outfits with quality stand-up harnesses. The terrain is not forgiving, and a hooked grander will test every knot, connection, and piece of hardware on your boat.

Striped Marlin

Striped marlin are more numerous than blacks and run 100 to 250 pounds through the season. They respond well to trolled lures in the 8 to 12-inch range and will eat pitch baits aggressively. Lighter tackle in the 30 to 50-pound class makes for excellent sport on stripers. They tend to feed higher in the water column than blacks, so watch for tailers and free-jumpers on calm days.

Yellowfin Tuna and Wahoo

Yellowfin tuna in the 30 to 150 pound range feed on the same bait concentrations that attract marlin. Chunk fishing with cut bonito over current edges produces steady action when the tuna are in. Wahoo patrol the shelf edge and respond to high-speed trolling with Yo-Zuri plugs and rigged ballyhoo on wire. The wahoo here run big, with 50 to 80 pound fish not uncommon.

Inshore and Light Tackle

Closer to shore, the rocky structure around Cabo Blanco holds corvina, snook, and various snapper species. Casting metal jigs and soft plastics from shore or a small panga puts you on fish without the expense of a full offshore charter. It's a solid option for rest days between offshore trips or for anglers traveling with non-fishing companions.

When to Go

  • January through April: Prime season. Water temperatures peak as the warm equatorial current pushes furthest south. Black marlin, striped marlin, and yellowfin tuna all peak during this window. February and March are historically the best months for big blacks. Seas are generally manageable.
  • May through July: Transition period. Striped marlin remain but black marlin numbers drop as water cools. Wahoo fishing picks up. Tuna stay consistent. Lower pressure and easier boat availability.
  • August through December: The Humboldt Current dominates, bringing cooler water temperatures. Billfish thin out significantly. Some yellowfin remain, and bottom fishing for snapper and grouper stays productive. This is the off-season for a reason, but budget-minded anglers can find fish and save money.

Where to Stay

Let's be straight about this: Cabo Blanco is remote and underdeveloped. It's a small fishing village in the Piura region of northern Peru, not a resort town. The old Cabo Blanco Fishing Club has been partially restored and offers basic accommodations with ocean views and deep history. A handful of small guesthouses and hostals operate in the village and nearby El Alto.

Mancora, about 30 minutes south, is Peru's most popular beach town and has a wider range of hotels, restaurants, and services. Staying in Mancora and driving to Cabo Blanco for fishing is a practical option, especially for trips that mix fishing with family time.

Getting there: Fly into Piura (Captain FAP Guillermo Concha Iberico Airport) from Lima. Piura to Cabo Blanco is about a two-hour drive north along the Pan-American Highway. Some anglers fly into Tumbes, which is closer but has fewer flight options. There is no public transit to Cabo Blanco worth mentioning. Arrange a rental car or private transfer through your lodge.

Charter boat availability is limited compared to major fishing destinations. Contact local operators well in advance. Boats here tend to be working pangas and converted commercial vessels rather than purpose-built sportfishers. Bring your own terminal tackle and be prepared for a rougher experience than you'd get in Costa Rica or Cabo San Lucas. The trade-off is fishing pressure measured in single digits of boats, not hundreds.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Black marlin: 80 to 130-pound stand-up outfits. Shimano Tiagra 80W or Penn International 80. Bent-butt rods with roller guides. 300 to 400-pound monofilament wind-on leaders. 12/0 to 14/0 forged hooks for large live baits.
  • Striped marlin: 30 to 50-pound conventional outfits. Medium trolling lures (8 to 12 inches) in purple/black and blue/white. 150 to 200-pound fluorocarbon leaders.
  • Yellowfin tuna: 50-pound conventional or heavy spinning gear. Butterfly jigs in 150 to 300 gram range. Chunk rigs with 80-pound fluorocarbon and 8/0 circle hooks.
  • Wahoo: Wire leader (single-strand #10 to #12) or heavy fluorocarbon (130-pound). High-speed trolling lures. Yo-Zuri Bonita in 8-inch size.
  • Terminal extras: Bring more than you think you need. Resupply options are essentially zero in Cabo Blanco. Extra hooks, leaders, crimps, swivels, and rigging supplies are worth their weight. A portable crimping kit is essential.
  • Safety gear: Satellite communicator (Garmin inReach or similar). Cell service is unreliable offshore. Let someone know your float plan every day.
