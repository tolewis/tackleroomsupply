Long Island is a 76-mile ribbon of land separating the shallow Bahama Bank from the deep Atlantic, and that geography makes it one of the most versatile fishing destinations in the country. The western shore has wadeable bonefish flats, the eastern coast drops into blue water within a mile of shore, and Flying Fish Marina at Clarence Town is the southernmost full-service marina in the Bahamas. If you want serious pelagic fishing combined with world-class flats in a place that still feels like the real Bahamas, this is it.

Why Long Island

The northern tip of Long Island sits where the Exuma Sound meets the Atlantic, and that convergence creates a funnel for every pelagic species moving through the central Bahamas. Blue marlin, white marlin, sailfish, wahoo, and yellowfin tuna all pass through this corridor. The drop-off along the east coast is steep and close to shore, which means 15-minute runs to deep water instead of the hour-plus transits you face at many other islands.

The western shore is a different world. Protected from the prevailing easterlies, the leeward flats stretch for miles with hard sand bottom and scattered turtle grass. Bonefish here average 3-5 lbs, with fish over 8 lbs common enough to keep things interesting. The town of Deadman's Cay sits roughly at the island's midpoint, and the flats both north and south of it produce consistently.

Flying Fish Marina at Clarence Town anchors the fishing infrastructure. It's a proper sportfishing marina with fuel, ice, tackle, charter boats, and dockside power. Cape Santa Maria at the northern tip offers a different flavor: luxury beachfront with access to some of the best bonefishing on the island.

How to Fish It

Offshore

The pelagic fishing off Long Island's northern tip and east coast is as good as it gets in the Bahamas. Blue marlin season runs March through July, with fish in the 200-600 lb range working the deep edges. White marlin and sailfish overlap, peaking in spring. Trolling rigged ballyhoo and lure spreads along the 100-fathom line is standard. Most charters run 50-80 lb stand-up tackle with a mix of lures and natural bait.

Wahoo patrol the eastern ledges from November through April. High-speed trolling at 14-18 knots with diving lures and wire leaders picks them up on the current edges. Yellowfin tuna show on the deeper structure south of the island, especially around the seamounts. Chunking with cut bonito or squid in the chum line, or vertical jigging with butterfly jigs, produces when the fish are down.

Dolphin ride the weed lines and current rips from April through September. When you find floating debris or a good weed patch, stop the boat, chum with cut bait, and pitch live pilchards or small jigs on light spinning tackle. A single piece of flotsam can hold a school all afternoon.

Flats

The western flats are the draw for sight-casting anglers. Incoming tide is the window, when bonefish move from the deeper channels onto the shallow feeding grounds. Most flats are firm enough to wade, and a full day on foot covering water is more productive than sitting in a skiff in many areas.

Fly anglers use 7-8 wt rods with floating tropical lines, 10-foot leaders to 12 lb tippet, and #4-6 Gotcha, Crazy Charlie, or Spawning Shrimp patterns. Pink and tan are the reliable colors. Spin anglers throw 1/8 oz jigheads with fresh or artificial shrimp on 8-10 lb braid with a 12 lb fluorocarbon leader. Barracuda are in every channel, so check your leader after every encounter.

Bottom Fishing

The reef system along the east coast holds yellowtail snapper, mutton snapper, Nassau grouper (in season), and hogfish. Drop cut bait or live grunts on 2/0-3/0 circle hooks in 40-80 feet for consistent bottom action. The deeper ledges in 100-200 feet produce amberjack and almaco jack on heavier tackle.

When to Go

  • March-May: Prime offshore window. Blue marlin, white marlin, and sailfish on the deep edges. Calm seas for offshore runs. Bonefish active on the flats.
  • June-July: Blue marlin peaks. Dolphin on the weed lines. Flats fishing stays solid early morning and late afternoon.
  • November-February: Wahoo season. Bonefish concentrated in cooler water. Fewer tourists. Weather can blow out offshore days, so plan for flexibility.
  • August-October: Hurricane season. Fish are present but weather dictates the schedule. Lower rates and empty flats if you catch the right window.

Where to Stay

Flying Fish Marina at Clarence Town is the angling hub. Dockside rooms, a restaurant, fuel, bait, ice, and the charter fleet right outside your door. It's the base for offshore fishing and southern flats access. Cape Santa Maria Beach Resort at the northern tip is a higher-end option with beachfront villas and direct access to the north-end flats and offshore grounds. Chez Pierre in Millers on the central west coast offers a quieter boutique experience with bonefishing out front. For budget, rental houses in Deadman's Cay or Salt Pond put you central to both the flats and the marina.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 50-80 lb stand-up combos, skirted lures, rigged ballyhoo, 8/0-10/0 circle hooks, 300 lb wind-on leaders
  • Wahoo: 50 lb class, high-speed trolling lures, single-strand wire or cable, 300+ yards of 80 lb braid
  • Sailfish/white marlin: 20-30 lb stand-up, rigged ballyhoo, 7/0 circle hooks, fluorocarbon leaders
  • Yellowfin tuna: 50 lb spinning or conventional, butterfly jigs, chunking rigs with 5/0-7/0 circles
  • Bonefishing (fly): 7-8 wt rod, floating tropical line, #4-6 Gotcha/Crazy Charlie/Spawning Shrimp
  • Bonefishing (spin): Light spinning, 8-10 lb braid, 12 lb fluoro leader, 1/8 oz jigs with shrimp
  • Bottom fishing: Medium conventional, 30-50 lb line, 2/0-3/0 circles, cut bait or live grunts
