Venice, Louisiana sits at the end of the road, literally. Highway 23 dead-ends into a maze of marsh, river channels, and open Gulf that produces some of the most diverse fishing on the planet. The Mississippi River dumps nutrient-rich freshwater into the Gulf of Mexico here, feeding a food chain that supports everything from 50-pound bull redfish in the marshes to 200-pound yellowfin tuna on the offshore oil rigs. No other fishing destination in the continental US puts this many species within reach of a single marina.

Why Venice

The Mississippi River outflow is the key. All that sediment and nutrient-loaded freshwater creates a massive plume that extends dozens of miles into the Gulf, feeding phytoplankton blooms that attract baitfish, which attract everything else. Combine that with hundreds of oil and gas platforms providing artificial reef structure in 200 to 3,000 feet of water, and you've got a year-round fishery with no real dead season.

Offshore, the oil rigs hold resident populations of yellowfin tuna, blackfin tuna, and amberjack. Blue marlin, white marlin, mahi-mahi, and wahoo cruise the deeper water beyond the rigs. Closer in, the river's South Pass and Southwest Pass channels hold tarpon, jack crevalle, and cobia. And the vast marsh system flanking the river is the best redfish habitat in North America, with schools of bull reds stacked in cuts and bays from September through March.

How to Fish It

Yellowfin Tuna on the Rigs

This is Venice's signature fishery. Oil platforms in 800 to 3,000 feet of water hold yellowfin tuna from 20 to over 200 pounds year-round, with peak action from March through September. The standard technique is chunking: anchoring or drifting near a rig and tossing cut yellowfin or skipjack chunks to create a chum slick. When the fish move into the slick, drop a chunk bait on a 60 to 80-pound fluorocarbon leader with a 7/0 circle hook and hold on. Popping cork rigs with live hardtails work well for blackfin and smaller yellowfin. For trophy-class fish over 150 pounds, live bait on a flat line behind the boat is the proven method.

Blue Water Billfish

The blue water edge sits 50 to 80 miles south of Venice, depending on current conditions. Blue marlin and white marlin show from May through September along the shelf edge and around the deep-water floating rigs. Standard Gulf trolling spreads with skirted ballyhoo and medium-sized lures cover the water. The DeSoto Canyon, southeast of Venice, concentrates current edges and bait, making it a prime trolling zone. Mahi-mahi and wahoo fill the box on the way to and from the marlin grounds.

Marsh Redfish

The Louisiana marsh is redfish paradise. From October through March, bull redfish in the 20 to 45 pound class stack up in the shallow bays, bayous, and channel edges around Venice. Sight-casting to tailing reds on a flood tide with a gold spoon or soft plastic on a jighead is as good as fishing gets. For bigger numbers, drifting cut mullet or crab under a popping cork through marsh cuts produces nonstop action. Light spinning tackle or an 8-weight fly rod turns these fish into a serious fight in shallow water.

Nearshore Mixed Bag

The river passes and nearshore rigs within 20 miles of Venice hold a rotating cast of species. Cobia run the rigs and buoys from March through May. Tarpon roll in the river passes during summer. Sheepshead, drum, and speckled trout fill in the calendar gaps. A nearshore trip is a smart play when weather pushes offshore plans, and the variety is hard to beat.

When to Go

  • April through June: Prime offshore window. Yellowfin tuna fishing peaks on the rigs. Mahi-mahi arrive in numbers. Cobia run nearshore. Blue marlin start showing on the shelf edge. Weather is generally stable with light winds.
  • July through September: Yellowfin stay strong. Blue marlin peak in July and August. Wahoo pick up on the deeper rigs. Afternoon thunderstorms are daily, so plan for early departures. Hurricane season is real. Watch forecasts and don't gamble on marginal weather windows.
  • October through December: Bull redfish invade the marshes in massive schools. The marsh bite is phenomenal from mid-October through Christmas. Offshore tuna fishing slows slightly but still produces on warmer days. This is the sweet spot for anglers who want redfish with an offshore option.
  • January through March: Redfish continue strong in the marsh. Yellowfin tuna fishing rebuilds as water warms in March. Sheepshead and drum are excellent on nearshore structure. February can be raw and windy, limiting offshore access.

Where to Stay

Venice Marina is the primary hub, sitting right at the end of Highway 23. Several lodges and charter operations base out of the marina complex. Tidewater Lodge and Venice Marina Lodge are the most popular angler accommodations, offering rooms, boat slips, and fish-cleaning stations.

Cypress Cove Marina and other smaller operations along the last stretch of Highway 23 offer alternatives with varying levels of amenity. Some are bare-bones camps with boat launches and bunk rooms. Others are full-service lodges with meals included.

Getting there: Fly into Louis Armstrong New Orleans International Airport (MSY). Venice is about 75 miles south, roughly a 90-minute drive straight down Highway 23 through Plaquemines Parish. The drive itself is an experience: levees, marsh, refineries, and shrimp boats the whole way. There are no restaurants, gas stations, or services in the last 15 miles, so fuel up and grab supplies in Buras or Port Sulphur on the way down.

Charter boats range from 24-foot bay boats for marsh redfish to 60-foot sportfishers for offshore marlin trips. Book well ahead for peak yellowfin season (April and May) and fall redfish season (October and November).

Tackle Recommendations

  • Yellowfin tuna (chunking): 50 to 80-pound conventional outfits. Shimano Talica 25 or Accurate Valiant 500. 7-foot stand-up rods. 80-pound fluorocarbon leaders, 7/0 to 8/0 circle hooks. Flat-fall jigs in 150 to 250 gram range for jigging the rigs.
  • Billfish: 30 to 50-pound trolling outfits for white marlin. 50 to 80-pound for blues. Medium skirted ballyhoo rigs and Ilander-style lures in blue/white and pink/white.
  • Redfish (marsh): 3000 to 4000-size spinning reels on 7-foot medium rods. 20-pound braid, 20 to 30-pound fluorocarbon leader. Gold spoons (Johnson Silver Minnow in gold), soft plastics on 1/4 to 3/8 oz jigheads (Matrix Shad in chartreuse/white), weedless spoons for grass flats.
  • Redfish (fly): 8-weight fly rod, floating line, 9-foot leader tapered to 16-pound. Spun deer hair flies, EP baitfish in chartreuse/white, Clouser Minnows in tan/white. Weed guards are mandatory in the marsh.
  • Cobia: Medium-heavy spinning or conventional gear. 40-pound braid. Live eels, large soft plastics, and heavy bucktail jigs in brown/white.
  • Bug spray. The marsh mosquitoes in Louisiana are legendary. Bring DEET-based repellent and a Thermacell. You'll need both.
