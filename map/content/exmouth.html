Exmouth sits at the tip of Western Australia's North West Cape, where the Ningaloo Reef runs right along the shoreline and the continental shelf drops into 3,000 feet of water within a mile of the beach. That combination of reef structure, deep access, and warm Leeuwin Current creates one of the most diverse big-game fisheries in the Southern Hemisphere. The first Australian blue marlin over 1,000 lbs was caught here in 2018, and the fishery has been building momentum ever since. This is frontier blue-water fishing with legitimate world-record potential.

Why Exmouth

Ningaloo Reef stretches 160 miles along the coast, and unlike the Great Barrier Reef, it's a fringing reef that sits right against the shore. Step off the beach and you're on the reef. Run 20 minutes offshore and you're in 1,000 feet of water over canyon structure. That proximity to deep water means pelagic species patrol within sight of land. Blue marlin, sailfish, yellowfin tuna, giant trevally, Spanish mackerel, and wahoo are all realistic targets in a single day.

The Leeuwin Current, a warm tropical current flowing south along the WA coast, delivers warm blue water and bait to the North West Cape from November through April. When that current is running strong, the fishing goes from good to absurd. Sailfish in triple-digit numbers per day have been recorded during peak billfish tournaments. GT in the 40-60 kg class cruise the reef edges. And the blue marlin fishery, still relatively young and underpressured, is producing bigger fish every season.

How to Fish It

Blue Marlin

The blue marlin bite centers on the Exmouth Canyon system, particularly along the western edge of the cape where the shelf break runs closest to shore. Trolling large skirted lures at 7-9 knots along the canyon walls and over the subsurface ridges is the standard approach. The water is deep and blue, and the marlin feed on schools of scad, flying fish, and skipjack that stage on the shelf break. Tackle is 80-130 lb class, matching what you'd use anywhere in the world for blues.

Sailfish

Exmouth sailfish are extraordinary. The fishery runs from February through July, with March through May being peak months. Live baiting with garfish, scad, and small mackerel on 15-30 lb spin tackle is the preferred method. The fish congregate along the reef edges and in the channels between reef systems. Multiple hookups on sailfish in a single session are common, and the Exmouth Game Fishing Club has documented days with 20+ releases per boat.

Giant Trevally

Ningaloo Reef is a GT factory. Fish over 100 lbs patrol the reef passes and bomboras (submerged reef heads). Popping with large stickbaits and cup-face poppers on heavy spinning tackle (PE8-10) is the method. Work the reef edges at dawn and dusk, targeting the passes where tidal flow concentrates bait. The coral structure is sharp and unforgiving, so expect some leader casualties. The GT here are aggressive, well-fed, and pull like freight trains.

Spanish Mackerel and Reef Species

Huge Spanish mackerel run along Ningaloo from March through September. Trolling Rapalas and bibbed minnow lures at 6-8 knots along the reef edge produces fish in the 20-40 lb range, with specimens over 60 lbs taken every season. The reef itself holds coral trout, rankin cod, red emperor, and spangled emperor on bottom-fishing rigs.

When to Go

  • November-December: Season building. Leeuwin Current pushing warm water south. First blue marlin and yellowfin showing on the shelf break. GT active on the reef.
  • January-February: Water temps peak at 82-84°F. Blue marlin bite building. Sailfish starting to show in numbers.
  • March-April: Prime time across all species. Peak sailfish, strong blue marlin, GT on the reef edges, Spanish mackerel starting their run. Exmouth Game Fishing Club tournaments run during this window.
  • May-July: Late sailfish. Spanish mackerel in full swing. Water cooling but still fishable. Whale shark season overlaps (bonus for non-fishing days).
  • August-October: Slowest period. Some bottom fishing and mackerel. Most charter operators use this time for maintenance.

Where to Stay

Exmouth is a small town (population around 2,500) built primarily around the fishing, diving, and whale shark tourism industries. Accommodations include caravan parks, holiday rentals, a few motels, and a handful of resort-style properties. This is not Cairns or Kona. Pack your expectations accordingly and you'll have a great time.

The Exmouth Game Fishing Club is the hub of the fishing community. Located at the Exmouth boat harbour, it has fuel, a boat ramp, cleaning tables, weigh-in facilities, and a bar where you can swap stories with locals who know the water. Most visiting anglers either trailer their own boats or book with local charter operators who run out of the harbour.

Getting to Exmouth requires a flight from Perth to Learmonth Airport (about 2.5 hours), then a 30-minute drive north to town. There are no direct international flights. Rental cars are essential since the town is spread out and the best shore-based fishing spots require a vehicle.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up tackle, large skirted trolling lures, 300 lb fluorocarbon leaders
  • Sailfish: 15-30 lb spinning rods, 4000-6000 size reels, 30 lb braid, 40-60 lb fluorocarbon leaders, 4/0-6/0 circle hooks
  • GT popping: Heavy spinning rods PE8-10, 16000-18000 size reels, 200 lb braid, 200 lb fluorocarbon leaders, large cup-face poppers and stickbaits to 200mm
  • Spanish mackerel: 30-50 lb trolling gear, wire traces or heavy fluorocarbon, bibbed minnow lures and Rapalas
  • Reef fishing: 30-50 lb conventional, paternoster rigs, 8/0 hooks, squid and cut bait
  • Sun protection: Australian UV is brutal. Long-sleeve shirts, quality polarized glasses, and serious sunscreen are mandatory, not optional
