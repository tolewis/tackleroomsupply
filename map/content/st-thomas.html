St. Thomas sits at the top of the Virgin Islands chain, and just 20 miles north of the island the ocean floor falls off a cliff. The North Drop is one of the most dramatic ledges in the Atlantic, where the bottom goes from 600 feet to over 6,000 feet in less than a mile. Blue marlin stack up on that edge like they're waiting in line.

Why St. Thomas

The North Drop consistently produces some of the largest blue marlin in the Caribbean. Fish over 500 lbs are caught every summer, and granders are a real possibility for anyone putting in the time. The ledge creates a current upwelling that concentrates baitfish (blackfin tuna, flying fish, squid), and the marlin follow. It's a textbook pelagic food chain compressed into a narrow strike zone.

The USVI Open/Atlantic Blue Marlin Tournament, held every August, is one of the premier billfish events in the world. Prize pools push past $1 million, and the fleet regularly raises double-digit marlin in three days of fishing. Beyond the tournament scene, St. Thomas also produces solid yellowfin tuna on the Drop, wahoo along the shelf edges, and mahi-mahi in the spring and fall.

Access is the other selling point. US passport, US currency, no customs hassle. You fly in like you're going to Florida, but the fishing is world-class bluewater.

How to Fish It

Blue Marlin

Trolling the North Drop edge with a mix of lures and rigged natural bait. Most St. Thomas boats run a 6-rod spread: Kona-style skirted lures on the long riggers and flat lines, with rigged ballyhoo or horse ballyhoo on the short corners. Trolling speed runs 7-9 knots, working the contour where 100 fathoms meets the abyss.

The key is staying on the edge. Captains who know the Drop have GPS waypoints marking every notch, bump, and cut in the ledge. The marlin patrol these features. Dredges and teasers are common, and a well-placed daisy chain of mullet or ballyhoo will pull marlin into the spread from a distance.

Tackle runs 50-130 lb depending on tournament rules and how serious you are about releasing big fish quickly. Most charter boats are rigged with 80 lb stand-up gear as the standard.

Yellowfin Tuna

Yellowfin in the 30-80 lb range show up on the North Drop from late spring through fall. Chunking with cut skipjack or bonito over the edge produces consistent bites, and poppers at dawn can be explosive when the tuna are pushing bait on the surface. Light tackle anglers will find 30 lb spinning gear and flat fall jigs productive when the fish are marked on the sounder.

Wahoo

Wahoo run the shelf edges south of St. Thomas, especially around the underwater ridges between St. Thomas and St. Croix. High-speed trolling at 12-16 knots with wahoo bombs, jet heads, and rigged ballyhoo behind planers or downriggers targets 30-60 lb fish. Best from November through March.

Mahi-Mahi

Mahi show up along the current edges and around floating debris from spring through early winter. Trolling small skirted lures and ballyhoo on 20-30 lb spinning or light conventional gear picks up schoolies and the occasional bull in the 30-40 lb range. When you find one, work the area. Where there's one mahi, there are usually more stacked underneath.

When to Go

  • June–September: Peak blue marlin season on the North Drop. August brings the USVI Open tournament and the heaviest concentration of boats and fish.
  • April–May: Transitional months. Early marlin showing up on the Drop, plus mahi-mahi and yellowfin building.
  • October–November: Late marlin bite. Wahoo starting to show. Fewer boats, still productive.
  • December–March: Wahoo season. Yellowfin tuna on the banks. Lighter boat traffic, calmer seas on the south side.
  • Year-round: Mahi-mahi, blackfin tuna, and reef species (yellowtail snapper, grouper) within a few miles of shore.

Where to Stay

Red Hook on the east end of St. Thomas is the main fishing hub. American Yacht Harbor has fuel, ice, and most of the charter fleet. Yacht Haven Grande in Charlotte Amalie is the other major marina, with bigger slips, tournament headquarters, and walking distance to town.

The run to the North Drop from Red Hook is roughly 20 miles, about an hour in a sport fisher. From Yacht Haven Grande, add another 15-20 minutes. Hotels and vacation rentals are plentiful on both ends of the island. Charlotte Amalie has more restaurants and nightlife; Red Hook puts you closer to the boats.

Flights from the East Coast run 3-4 hours nonstop. Cyril E. King Airport (STT) handles major carriers. No passport needed for US citizens, and the dollar is the local currency. Ground transport is taxis and rental cars; the island is small enough that nothing is more than a 30-minute drive.

Charter rates run $1,800-3,000/day for a full offshore trip. During tournament weeks, book well in advance. The fleet fills up months out.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 50-130 lb stand-up or chair tackle (provided by charter), skirted lures, rigged horse ballyhoo, circle hooks for release
  • Yellowfin tuna: 30-50 lb spinning or conventional, flat fall jigs (200-300g), poppers, chunking rigs with 60 lb fluorocarbon
  • Wahoo: 30-50 lb conventional, high-speed trolling lures, #10 wire or 130 lb fluorocarbon leader
  • Mahi-mahi: 20-30 lb spinning, live pilchards or ballyhoo, light wire or 40 lb fluorocarbon
  • Reef: 15-30 lb spinning, jigs and cut bait, 30-40 lb fluorocarbon leader
