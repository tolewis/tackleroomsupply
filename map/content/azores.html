The Azores are nine volcanic islands dropped in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean, 900 miles west of Portugal, sitting directly on the Mid-Atlantic Ridge. The underwater topography here is violent and dramatic: seamounts rising from 10,000 feet to within 100 feet of the surface, deep ridges, and volcanic pinnacles that create upwelling zones loaded with bait and blue-water predators. Fourteen blue marlin over 1,000 lbs have been landed in these waters, and the Condor Bank seamount complex is one of the most productive deep-sea fishing grounds in the Atlantic.

Why the Azores

Geography makes this fishery. The Azores sit at the junction of the Gulf Stream, the North Atlantic Current, and the Canary Current. Warm water, cold water, and the volcanic seamount structure create a mixing zone that concentrates marine life on a massive scale. The Condor Bank, a submerged seamount rising from abyssal depths to about 600 feet below the surface southwest of Faial Island, is the crown jewel. Bigeye tuna, blue marlin, and blue sharks stack up on Condor Bank in numbers that European anglers have built entire fishing careers around.

The fishery is split between two equally compelling targets. Blue marlin migrate through from June through September, with fish averaging 400-700 lbs and granders appearing regularly enough to keep the heavy tackle rigged at all times. Bigeye tuna are present from May through October, with fish routinely exceeding 200 lbs. Some boats specifically target bigeye on the seamount drops and consider the marlin a bonus. Either way, you're fishing over extraordinary structure in deep, clean Atlantic water.

How to Fish It

Blue Marlin Trolling

The Azores fleet trolls skirted lures along the seamount edges and through the channels between islands. Standard spreads are 5-6 lures at 7-9 knots, working the depth contours where the seamounts create current disruption and bait concentrations. The captains fish specific waypoints on Condor Bank and the Princess Alice Bank, reading the sonar for bait schools and temperature breaks. Tackle is 80-130 lb class, and the marlin here are strong, well-fed fish that fight hard in deep water with plenty of room to run.

Lure selection favors medium to large jet-head and plunger-style designs, 10-14 inches, in standard Atlantic color patterns: blue/white, black/purple, pink/white. The volcanic water color is a deep indigo that makes darker lure patterns stand out well.

Bigeye Tuna

This is world-class bigeye fishing. The primary method is deep drifting with chunk bait over the seamounts at night. Boats anchor or drift over the Condor Bank at sunset, set up a chum slick with cut mackerel or squid, and drop baits on circle hooks to 200-600 foot depths. When the bigeye move up the water column to feed, the bite can be explosive. Fish in the 150-300 lb range are common, with occasional monsters over 350 lbs.

Daytime bigeye fishing involves slow-trolling live bait or dead baits on downriggers along the seamount contours. Some boats combine marlin lure trolling on the surface with deep lines for bigeye, covering both targets simultaneously.

Other Species

Yellowfin tuna, skipjack, wahoo, and white marlin are all present during the summer months. Blue sharks are abundant on the seamounts and provide fast-action light-tackle fishing for anglers who want rod-bending time between marlin bites. The islands also hold some fantastic shore-based fishing for yellowmouth barracuda, amberjack, and various reef species from the volcanic rock ledges.

When to Go

  • May-June: Season building. Bigeye tuna arriving on the seamounts. First blue marlin of the season. Water temps climbing through low 70s.
  • July-August: Peak season for blue marlin and bigeye tuna. Water temps 74-78°F. Condor Bank at its most productive. This is tournament season and boats need to be booked early.
  • September: Late-season marlin, excellent bigeye tuna bite. Fewer boats, great fishing. Weather can get unpredictable as autumn approaches.
  • October: Bigeye still biting on the seamounts. Marlin numbers dropping. Last realistic window before the fleet wraps up.
  • November-April: Off-season. Weather is rough, water is cold, and the pelagic species have moved on. Some bottom fishing available but the charter fleet is largely idle.

Where to Stay

Horta, on Faial Island, is the traditional base for Azores big-game fishing. The Horta Marina is one of the most famous transatlantic sailing stopovers in the world, and it supports a well-equipped sport fishing fleet. Fuel, water, ice, and basic marine supplies are available. The town itself is a charming Portuguese harbor community with restaurants, small hotels, and guesthouses. Peter Cafe Sport, the legendary sailors' bar on the waterfront, has been a gathering point for fishermen and ocean crossers since 1918.

Ponta Delgada on Sao Miguel Island is the largest city and the main international gateway, with direct flights from Lisbon, London, and several US East Coast cities (Boston and New York via SATA or Azores Airlines). From Ponta Delgada, inter-island flights or ferries connect to Faial. Some charter operations also run out of Sao Miguel, particularly targeting the seamounts south and east of the island.

Accommodations across the Azores are modest but comfortable. Boutique hotels, guesthouses, and vacation rentals are the norm. The islands are a Portuguese territory, so the food is excellent, the wine is local, and the pace of life is slow. Non-fishing companions will find plenty to do with volcanic hot springs, whale watching, vineyard tours, and dramatic hiking trails across the calderas.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Blue marlin: 80-130 lb stand-up tackle, two-speed lever drag reels, 130 lb mono or hollow-core braid (most charters provide gear)
  • Marlin lures: Skirted trolling lures 10-14 inches, jet heads and plungers, blue/white, black/purple, pink/white
  • Bigeye tuna: 80 lb stand-up rods, 6/0-8/0 circle hooks, 150-200 lb fluorocarbon leaders, glow sticks and light sticks for deep drops
  • Chunk bait rigs: 3-way swivels, 8-16 oz bank sinkers, 6 feet of 150 lb fluorocarbon to circle hook, fresh mackerel and squid for bait
  • Light tackle: 30-50 lb spinning for yellowfin, wahoo, and blue sharks. Metal jigs 100-300g for seamount jigging.
  • Bring your own: Wind-on leaders, quality crimps, and preferred terminal tackle. Tackle availability in the Azores is improving but still limited compared to major US or Australian ports
