From Key Largo to Key West, the Florida Keys stretch 120 miles through some of the most diverse fisheries in the Western Hemisphere. Offshore billfish, flats bonefish, bridge tarpon, reef snapper, and backcountry redfish — it's all here, and most of it is within 20 minutes of the dock.

Why the Keys

The Keys sit between the Atlantic and the Gulf of Mexico, with the Gulf Stream running close on the ocean side. That warm current carries sailfish south along the reef from November through April — the "sailfish alley" bite between Fort Pierce and Islamorada is legendary. Meanwhile, the backcountry (Florida Bay side) holds tarpon, bonefish, permit, and redfish on shallow flats year-round.

Islamorada calls itself the "Sportfishing Capital of the World" — and the charter fleet backs it up. Key West has its own world-class fishery: light-tackle yellowtail snapper, mutton snapper, permit on the wrecks, and sailfish on the humps.

How to Fish It

Sailfishing

Kite fishing is the Keys technique. Live goggle-eyes, pilchards, or threadfins are suspended from kites on the surface while the boat drifts the reef edge. When a sail comes up and takes the bait, you let it eat, come tight, and the fight is on. Most charters run 2-3 kites with 4-6 baits in the water.

Tackle: 20-30 lb spinning or conventional, circle hooks, fluorocarbon leader. Sailfish are almost exclusively catch-and-release in the Keys.

Flats Fishing

Bonefish, permit, and tarpon — the "Grand Slam" — on a single day. It's rare but it happens here more than anywhere else. Fly or spin, a poled skiff on the flats of Islamorada, Marathon, or the Lower Keys is bucket-list fishing.

  • Bonefish: 7-8 wt fly or light spinning, shrimp patterns, incoming tide
  • Permit: 9-10 wt fly or medium spinning, live crabs or Merkin crab patterns
  • Tarpon: 10-12 wt fly or 20-30 lb spinning, live mullet under the bridges at night, or sight-casting on the flats in May

Reef & Wreck

Yellowtail snapper on light tackle over the reef is a Keys staple — and delicious. Mutton snapper on the full moon, grouper on the wrecks, and cobia around channel markers round out the inshore calendar.

When to Go

  • November–April: Sailfish season. Bonefish and permit strong on the flats.
  • April–June: Tarpon migration. Giant tarpon up to 150 lbs stack in the channels and under bridges.
  • July–October: Mahi, tuna, and wahoo offshore. Reef fishing strong. Permit peak on the wrecks.
  • Year-round: Yellowtail snapper, bonefish, redfish in the backcountry.

Where to Stay

Islamorada (MM 80-84) is the epicenter for flats fishing and sailfishing. Bud N' Mary's Marina is the oldest and most famous charter dock. Marathon (MM 50) offers great access to both ocean and backcountry. Key West has the biggest nightlife and the most diverse offshore fleet.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Sailfishing: 20-30 lb spinning or conventional, circle hooks, live bait under kites
  • Bonefish: 7-8 wt fly rod, floating line, #4-6 Gotcha or shrimp patterns
  • Tarpon: 10-12 wt fly or heavy spinning, 60-80 lb leader, live mullet or crab
  • Yellowtail snapper: Light spinning, 10-15 lb fluro, small hooks, chum bag
