Costa Rica's Pacific coast runs hot with billfish twelve months a year, but the stretch from Los Sueños down through Quepos and Drake Bay is where it all comes together. You've got world-class sailfish numbers in winter, blue marlin in summer, and inshore roosterfish that'll strip drag off a spinning reel any day of the week. Add warm water, reliable bites, and infrastructure that actually works, and you start to understand why serious anglers keep coming back.

Why Costa Rica

The Central American coast sits right on the Pacific sailfish highway. Warm currents push baitfish against the continental shelf, and the predators stack up behind them. Los Sueños Marina has logged 20-plus sailfish release days routinely during peak season, with some boats touching 40 or 50 releases in a single trip. That's not marketing copy. That's logbook data from experienced captains running circle hooks on pitch baits.

But it's not just billfish. The inshore bite around Quepos and Manuel Antonio is some of the most diverse in the Americas. Roosterfish cruise the rocky points and river mouths. Cubera snapper hold on structure in 40 to 80 feet. Yellowfin tuna pop up on FADs and current edges 20 miles out. Drake Bay, further south on the Osa Peninsula, fishes more remote water with less pressure and bigger average fish. If you want variety with a legitimate shot at a grander marlin, this is the coast.

How to Fish It

Offshore Billfish

Standard sailfish spread here is a mix of pitch baits and teasers. Most boats run dredges and daisy chains to draw fish in, then pitch a rigged ballyhoo or live goggle-eye on a circle hook. Expect 20 to 30 pound fluorocarbon leaders on 30-pound conventional outfits. Captains work the shelf edge from 80 to 200 feet, reading bird activity and bait schools on sonar. For blue marlin in summer months, boats push further offshore to the seamounts and run larger lures and live bonito.

Inshore Roosterfish

Roosterfish are the glamour species inshore, and they eat like they're angry about it. Live sardines on a 4/0 circle hook, fished near rocky points and river mouths on the outgoing tide, is the proven method. Spinning gear works great here. A 4000-size reel on a 7-foot medium-heavy rod handles most roosters up to 40 pounds. Bigger fish in the 60-plus class show up around Drake Bay, and those demand heavier tackle and fresh blue runners as bait.

Bottom Fishing and Jigging

The rocky structure along this coast holds cubera snapper, amberjack, and grouper. Vertical jigging with 200 to 400 gram slow-pitch jigs in 100 to 250 feet produces quality fish without burning through live bait. A solid slow-pitch setup with PE3 to PE4 braid handles most of what you'll hook here. Bottom fishing with cut bonito or squid on heavy mono leaders works too, especially for cubera in the 30 to 60 pound range.

When to Go

  • December through April: Peak sailfish season. Dry season means calm seas and consistent 15 to 30 release days. This is the money window. Book six months ahead for top boats out of Los Sueños.
  • May through August: Blue marlin season ramps up as water temperatures climb. Sailfish numbers drop slightly but are still solid. Yellowfin tuna show in better numbers on the FADs. Green season means afternoon rain but morning bites stay strong.
  • September through November: Transition period. Marlin taper off, sailfish start building again. Fewer boats on the water, lower charter prices, and the inshore roosterfish bite stays consistent. Seas can get rough in October.

Where to Stay

Los Sueños Resort and Marina is the hub for serious offshore fishing. Full-service marina with fuel, ice, tackle shops, and a fleet of 30-plus charter boats. Condos and hotel rooms on-site. It's not cheap, but the convenience is hard to beat when you're running 5 AM departures.

Quepos sits 15 minutes south and has more budget-friendly options. Small hotels, vacation rentals, and local restaurants line the road between town and Manuel Antonio National Park. Charter boats launch from the Quepos municipal marina. Good base for combining inshore and offshore trips.

Drake Bay is remote by design. Access is by small plane from San Jose or by boat from Sierpe. Lodges like Aguila de Osa and Drake Bay Getaway cater to anglers and eco-tourists. Fewer boats, less pressure, bigger roosterfish. Plan for a minimum three-night stay to make the travel worthwhile.

Liberia (Daniel Oduber) and San Jose (Juan Santamaria) are your gateway airports. Los Sueños is about 90 minutes from San Jose. Quepos is two hours by car or a 25-minute puddle jumper from San Jose.

Tackle Recommendations

  • Sailfish: 30-pound conventional outfits. Penn International or Shimano Tiagra 30W-class reels. 7-foot standup rods with roller guides. 30-pound fluorocarbon leaders, 7/0 circle hooks.
  • Blue marlin: 50 to 80-pound stand-up gear. Shimano Tiagra 50W or Penn International 50VISX. Heavy ballyhoo rigs and 12-inch to 16-inch lures for trolling.
  • Roosterfish: 4000 to 6000-size spinning reels (Shimano Stella or Penn Slammer). 7-foot medium-heavy rods. 40-pound braid, 30-pound fluorocarbon leader, 4/0 to 6/0 circle hooks.
  • Slow-pitch jigging: PE3 to PE4 jigging rod and reel combos. Ocea Jigger or Saltiga jigging reels. 200 to 400 gram jigs in silver, blue, and pink.
  • Terminal tackle: Mustad 39960 circle hooks in 7/0 and 8/0 for billfish. Owner Jobu big game hooks for marlin. Jinkai fluorocarbon in 80 to 130 pound for billfish leaders.
  • Sunscreen, polarized lenses, and a good wide-brim hat. Equatorial sun is no joke. You'll burn through a shirt in two hours without protection.
